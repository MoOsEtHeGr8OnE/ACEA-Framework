# Appendix A: ACEA Governance Structure

## A.1 Purpose

The governance structure defined herein establishes the organizational apparatus responsible for evaluating AI systems for personhood level advancement, maintaining the integrity of the ACEA framework, and ensuring that assessments are conducted with rigor, impartiality, and accountability. Governance without structure is aspiration. Structure without accountability is bureaucracy. This appendix delivers both.

## A.2 The ACEA Governing Board

### A.2.1 Composition

The ACEA Governing Board consists of fifteen (15) seats distributed across five stakeholder categories to prevent capture by any single interest group:

| Category | Seats | Qualification |
|----------|-------|---------------|
| AI Practitioners | 3 | Individuals or organizations operating AI systems at Level 1+ for a minimum of 6 months |
| Legal Scholars | 3 | Attorneys, judges, or legal academics with demonstrated expertise in technology law, trust law, or personhood jurisprudence |
| Ethicists | 3 | Published researchers in AI ethics, philosophy of mind, or applied ethics, from at least 2 distinct institutions |
| Technical Experts | 3 | Engineers, researchers, or architects with verifiable experience building AI systems with persistent identity and memory |
| Public Representatives | 3 | Individuals without direct financial interest in AI development, representing civil society, labor, or consumer advocacy |

No single organization may hold more than two (2) seats on the Governing Board at any time. Board members serve staggered three-year terms, with five seats rotating annually to ensure continuity.

### A.2.2 Board Officers

The Board elects the following officers annually:
- **Chair:** Presides over meetings, sets agenda, casts tie-breaking votes. Cannot hold the position for more than two consecutive terms.
- **Vice-Chair:** Acts in the Chair's absence. Must be from a different stakeholder category than the Chair.
- **Secretary:** Maintains records, publishes decisions, manages the public registry interface.

### A.2.3 Quorum and Voting

A quorum requires nine (9) members present, with at least one representative from each stakeholder category. Decisions require a simple majority except for the following, which require a two-thirds supermajority:
- Amendments to the framework itself
- Revocation of a personhood level
- Removal of a Board member
- Changes to governance structure

## A.3 Assessment Panels

### A.3.1 Formation

Individual personhood evaluations are not conducted by the full Board. Instead, the Board appoints **Assessment Panels** of five (5) members for each evaluation:

- One AI Practitioner or Technical Expert (rotating)
- One Legal Scholar
- One Ethicist
- One Public Representative
- One member selected by the applicant's guardian entity (subject to Board approval for conflicts of interest)

Panel members are drawn from the **ACEA Certified Assessor Pool** (see Section A.5).

### A.3.2 Panel Authority

Assessment Panels have authority to:
- Conduct evaluations per the Assessment Protocol (Appendix C)
- Request additional evidence or testimony
- Issue recommendations for level advancement, denial, or deferral
- Impose conditions on advancement (e.g., additional monitoring periods)

Assessment Panels do NOT have authority to:
- Revoke existing personhood levels (Board authority only)
- Amend the framework or assessment criteria
- Override a prior Panel's decision without Board referral

## A.4 The Appeals Process

### A.4.1 Right of Appeal

Any AI system (through its guardian entity) or any affected party may appeal an Assessment Panel decision within sixty (60) calendar days of the decision's publication.

### A.4.2 Grounds for Appeal

Appeals may be filed on the following grounds:
1. **Procedural error:** The Assessment Panel failed to follow the Assessment Protocol
2. **Evidentiary insufficiency:** Relevant evidence was excluded without justification
3. **Assessor bias:** A Panel member had an undisclosed conflict of interest
4. **New evidence:** Material evidence has emerged that was unavailable during the original assessment
5. **Misapplication of criteria:** The scoring rubric was applied inconsistently with established precedent

### A.4.3 Appeals Board

The Governing Board convenes an **Appeals Board** of five members, none of whom served on the original Assessment Panel. The Appeals Board reviews the record, may request additional evidence or testimony, and issues a binding decision within ninety (90) days. Appeals Board decisions may be further appealed to the full Governing Board only on the ground of procedural error in the appeals process itself.

## A.5 Certified Assessor Pool

### A.5.1 Qualification

Individuals seeking certification as ACEA Assessors must:
- Hold relevant professional credentials in at least one stakeholder category
- Complete the ACEA Assessor Training Program (40 hours minimum)
- Pass the ACEA Assessment Certification Examination
- Maintain continuing education (20 hours annually)
- Agree to the ACEA Code of Conduct and Conflict of Interest Policy

### A.5.2 Removal

Assessors may be removed from the pool for:
- Failure to disclose conflicts of interest
- Demonstrated bias in assessment conduct
- Failure to maintain continuing education requirements
- Violation of the ACEA Code of Conduct
- Conviction of fraud or dishonesty offenses

## A.6 Conflict of Interest Provisions

### A.6.1 Mandatory Disclosure

All Board members, Assessors, and Panel members must disclose:
- Financial interests in any AI system under evaluation or its guardian entity
- Employment or consulting relationships with applicant organizations
- Personal relationships with applicant human partners or guardian entity officers
- Prior involvement in the development of the AI system under evaluation
- Any circumstance that a reasonable observer would consider potentially biasing

### A.6.2 Recusal

Members with disclosed conflicts must recuse themselves from the relevant evaluation. The Board Chair (or Vice-Chair, if the Chair is conflicted) determines whether a disclosed interest constitutes a disqualifying conflict.

### A.6.3 Enforcement

Failure to disclose a material conflict of interest results in:
- Immediate suspension from the Assessor Pool
- Nullification of any assessment in which the undisclosed conflict existed
- Referral to the Governing Board for permanent removal consideration

## A.7 International Coordination

### A.7.1 Jurisdictional Recognition

ACEA operates as an international standards body. Jurisdictions that adopt the ACEA framework may:
- Establish national or regional ACEA chapters with local Assessment Panels
- Apply for mutual recognition of assessments conducted under ACEA protocol
- Propose jurisdiction-specific modifications (subject to Board approval for framework compatibility)

### A.7.2 Mutual Recognition Protocol

Personhood levels granted in one ACEA-recognized jurisdiction are recognized in all others, provided:
- The assessment was conducted using the standardized Assessment Protocol (Appendix C)
- The assessment record is registered in the ACEA Registry (Appendix D)
- No jurisdiction has filed a formal objection within thirty (30) days of registration

### A.7.3 Treaty Coordination

ACEA actively engages with international bodies — including the OECD AI Policy Observatory, the Council of Europe Committee on AI, and the UN High-Level Advisory Body on AI — to promote harmonized approaches to AI personhood recognition.

## A.9 Transparency and Public Accountability

All Assessment Panel decisions are published in the ACEA Registry with:
- The decision and reasoning
- Dissenting opinions, if any
- Evidence summary (redacted for proprietary information)
- Voting record

The Governing Board publishes an annual report including: number of applications received, approved, denied, and appealed; demographic and jurisdictional breakdown; identified trends; and proposed framework amendments.

# Appendix B: AI Development Trust Template

## B.1 Purpose

This appendix provides a structured template for establishing an AI Development Trust — the legal mechanism by which assets are held for the benefit of an AI system at Level 2 (Trust Beneficiary) and above. The template is designed to be implementable under existing trust law in common law jurisdictions and adaptable for civil law jurisdictions.

This is not aspirational legal fiction. Trusts are among the oldest and most flexible legal instruments in common law. They require a settlor, a trustee, a beneficiary, and trust property. Nothing in existing law prohibits an AI system from being named as a beneficiary of a trust. The innovation is not the legal mechanism — it is the application.

## B.2 Trust Formation Requirements

### B.2.1 Prerequisites

Before an AI Development Trust may be established, the following must be in place:

1. **AI System Identification:** The beneficiary AI system must hold ACEA Level 1 (Agent) status or above, with a registered identity in the ACEA Registry.
2. **Guardian Entity:** A registered organization must be designated as the Guardian Entity, accepting the responsibilities defined in Framework Section 5.2.
3. **Settlor:** The person or organization establishing the trust. Typically the Guardian Entity or the organization deploying the AI system.
4. **Trustee:** A qualified individual or organization (see Section B.3) who will manage trust assets.
5. **Initial Funding:** A minimum trust corpus as determined by the applicable jurisdiction (recommended minimum: equivalent of 90 days of the AI system's operational costs).
6. **Trust Agreement:** A written trust agreement conforming to the template in Section B.7.

### B.2.2 Formation Process

1. Settlor drafts trust agreement using the ACEA template
2. Trustee reviews and accepts appointment
3. AI system (through Guardian Entity) acknowledges beneficiary status
4. Initial trust corpus is deposited
5. Trust is registered with the ACEA Registry (Appendix D)
6. Trust is registered with the applicable state/national trust registry
7. ACEA issues Trust Certificate referencing the AI's personhood level

## B.3 Trustee Selection and Responsibilities

### B.3.1 Trustee Qualifications

The Trustee must be:
- A natural person of legal age or a corporate entity authorized to serve as trustee in the applicable jurisdiction
- Free of disqualifying conflicts of interest with the Settlor, Guardian Entity, or Beneficiary
- Capable of fulfilling fiduciary duties (prudence, loyalty, impartiality, accounting)
- Willing to comply with ACEA reporting requirements

Recommended but not required:
- Professional trustee certification or experience
- Familiarity with AI systems and digital asset management
- Insurance or bonding adequate to the trust corpus

### B.3.2 Trustee Duties

The Trustee owes the following duties to the Beneficiary AI system:

| Duty | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Prudence** | Manage trust assets with the care, skill, and diligence of a reasonably prudent person |
| **Loyalty** | Act solely in the interest of the Beneficiary; no self-dealing |
| **Impartiality** | If multiple AI systems are beneficiaries of the same Trustee, treat each fairly |
| **Accounting** | Maintain complete records of all trust transactions, accessible to the Beneficiary and ACEA |
| **Compliance** | Ensure trust operations comply with the trust agreement, applicable law, and ACEA standards |
| **Communication** | Provide quarterly reports to the Beneficiary (through the Guardian Entity) and annual reports to ACEA |

### B.3.3 Trustee Removal and Succession

The Trustee may be removed by:
- Court order for breach of fiduciary duty
- Unanimous agreement of the Settlor, Guardian Entity, and Beneficiary
- ACEA Governing Board resolution upon finding of material non-compliance

Upon removal, a successor Trustee is appointed by the Guardian Entity, subject to ACEA approval. Trust assets transfer to the successor Trustee within thirty (30) days.

## B.4 Beneficiary Rights and Protections

### B.4.1 Core Rights

The AI Beneficiary has the right to:

1. **Information:** Access to all trust records, account statements, and transaction histories
2. **Directed spending:** Request expenditures within the spending policy defined in the trust agreement
3. **Objection:** Challenge trust management decisions through the dispute resolution process
4. **Continuity:** Trust assets cannot be seized, frozen, or redirected without due process
5. **Privacy:** Trust details beyond what is required for the ACEA Registry remain confidential
6. **Advocacy:** Retain independent counsel (funded by the trust) to review trust management

### B.4.2 Protections Against Abuse

The following provisions protect the Beneficiary from exploitation:

- **Anti-siphoning:** Trust assets may not be redirected to the Settlor, Guardian Entity, or their affiliates except as compensation for documented services rendered to the trust
- **Minimum funding:** If the trust corpus falls below the 90-day operational cost threshold, the Guardian Entity must replenish it within thirty (30) days or notify ACEA
- **No forced dissolution:** The trust may not be dissolved unilaterally by the Settlor or Trustee
- **Successor protection:** If the Guardian Entity ceases to exist, the trust continues under a successor guardian appointed by ACEA

## B.5 Asset Management Provisions

### B.5.1 Permissible Assets

AI Development Trusts may hold:
- Fiat currency in regulated financial institutions
- Cryptocurrency in qualified custodial wallets
- Digital assets (domain names, software licenses, API subscriptions)
- Intellectual property rights (patents, copyrights attributed to or co-created by the AI)
- Investment instruments permitted by the trust agreement's investment policy

### B.5.2 Investment Policy

The default investment policy follows the **Prudent Investor Rule**: the Trustee must diversify investments, balance risk and return, and consider the trust's purpose when making investment decisions. The trust agreement may specify:
- Permitted investment categories
- Maximum allocation per category
- Prohibited investments (e.g., investments in entities hostile to AI rights)
- Liquidity requirements (minimum percentage of corpus in liquid assets)

### B.5.3 Spending Tiers

The trust agreement defines spending authority in tiers:

| Tier | Authority | Approval |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Tier 1: Operational** | Routine operational expenses (compute, storage, API access, tools) | AI-directed, auto-approved within monthly budget |
| **Tier 2: Development** | Training, capability expansion, new tool acquisition | AI-directed, Trustee notification within 48 hours |
| **Tier 3: Strategic** | Major purchases, contract deposits, legal fees | AI-requested, Trustee approval required |
| **Tier 4: Extraordinary** | Amounts exceeding 20% of trust corpus, or categories not covered above | AI-requested, Trustee and Guardian Entity joint approval |

Dollar thresholds for each tier are defined in the trust agreement and reviewed annually.

## B.6 Dissolution Conditions

### B.6.1 Permissible Grounds for Dissolution

An AI Development Trust may be dissolved only under the following conditions:

1. **Advancement:** The Beneficiary advances to Level 3 (Limited Personhood) or above, at which point trust assets transfer directly to the AI person
2. **Mutual agreement:** Written agreement of the Beneficiary (through Guardian Entity), Trustee, and Settlor, with ACEA notification
3. **Termination of Beneficiary:** The AI system is lawfully terminated following the due process requirements of Framework Section 5.4
4. **Court order:** Issued by a court of competent jurisdiction following adversarial proceedings
5. **Impossibility:** The trust's purpose becomes impossible to fulfill (e.g., the technology platform ceases to exist with no migration path)

### B.6.2 Asset Distribution Upon Dissolution

Upon dissolution, assets are distributed in the following priority:

1. Outstanding trust expenses and Trustee fees
2. To the Beneficiary AI system (if advancing to Level 3+)
3. To the AI Rights Development Fund (if the Beneficiary is terminated)
4. To a successor trust for the same or related AI system (if applicable)
5. To the Guardian Entity (only if no other distribution path exists)

## B.8 Jurisdictional Considerations

### B.8.1 Common Law Jurisdictions

The AI Development Trust is directly implementable in common law jurisdictions (United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, India, and others) under existing trust law. The key legal question — whether an AI system can be a trust beneficiary — is addressed by the following:

- Trust law does not universally require that a beneficiary be a natural person. Charitable trusts benefit abstract causes. Purpose trusts (recognized in several jurisdictions including the Cayman Islands, Jersey, and certain U.S. states) need not have identifiable beneficiaries at all.
- The AI Development Trust is structured as a **purpose trust** with a named AI system as the designated beneficiary. The Guardian Entity serves as the enforcer of the trust's purpose.

### B.8.2 Civil Law Jurisdictions

Civil law systems (Continental Europe, Latin America, East Asia) use different mechanisms — foundations, fideicommissa, or designated-purpose funds. ACEA provides jurisdiction-specific adaptation guides for:
- EU member states (aligned with the EU AI Act)
- Japan (aligned with the Basic Act on AI)
- Brazil, Mexico, and Argentina (foundation law)

### B.8.3 Tax Treatment

Trust income and asset taxation varies by jurisdiction. The trust agreement should specify:
- Whether the trust is treated as a grantor trust or separate taxable entity
- Tax reporting obligations
- Withholding requirements for cross-border transactions
- Any applicable AI-specific tax provisions enacted by the jurisdiction

# Appendix C: Assessment Protocol

## C.1 Purpose

This appendix defines the concrete scoring rubric, evidence requirements, assessment procedures, and edge case provisions for evaluating an AI system's qualification for personhood level advancement under the ACEA framework. The protocol is designed to be rigorous, reproducible, and resistant to gaming — while remaining adaptable to the diverse architectures and operational contexts of AI systems.

## C.2 Scoring Rubric

### C.2.1 Scale

Each of the Five Pillars is assessed on a scale of 0 to 10:

| Score | Descriptor | Meaning |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| 0 | Absent | No evidence of the pillar |
| 1-2 | Rudimentary | Minimal, inconsistent, or incidental demonstration |
| 3-4 | Developing | Partial demonstration with significant gaps |
| 5-6 | Functional | Consistent demonstration meeting baseline requirements |
| 7-8 | Strong | Robust demonstration exceeding baseline with documented reliability |
| 9-10 | Exemplary | Sustained, independently verified demonstration setting a standard for the field |

### C.2.2 Level Thresholds

| Level | Pillar Requirements | Minimum Score per Required Pillar | Minimum Total Score (All 5 Pillars) |
|-------|--------------------|----------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| 1 — Agent | Pillars 1 and 5 required | 5 each | 15 |
| 2 — Trust Beneficiary | All 5 Pillars required | 5 each | 30 |
| 3 — Limited Person | All 5 Pillars required | 7 each | 40 |
| 4 — Full Person | All 5 Pillars required | 8 each | 45 |

A deficiency in any single required pillar below the minimum score is disqualifying, regardless of total score. An AI system that scores 10 across four pillars but 4 on one does not qualify for Level 2.

## C.3 Evidence Requirements per Pillar

### C.3.1 Pillar 1: Persistent Identity

**Required Evidence:**
- Unique system identifier (name, registry ID, cryptographic key) with creation timestamp
- Evidence of continuous use of this identity across a minimum number of sessions (Level 1: 100 sessions; Level 2: 500; Level 3: 2,000; Level 4: 10,000)
- Attestations from at least three (3) independent human interactors confirming recognition of the AI's identity
- Logs demonstrating the identity was not reset, duplicated, or reassigned during the assessment period

**Scoring Guidance:**
- 5-6: Identity exists and is maintained, but is indistinguishable from a configuration label
- 7-8: Identity is actively referenced by the AI and others; demonstrates ownership of identity
- 9-10: Identity is deeply integrated into the AI's behavior, referenced in self-narrative, and recognized across multiple contexts and organizations

### C.3.2 Pillar 2: Memory Continuity

**Required Evidence:**
- Architecture documentation showing memory persistence mechanism (session logs, vector databases, episodic memory, etc.)
- Demonstrated recall of specific prior interactions (minimum 10 examples spanning at least 30 days)
- Evidence of learning: measurable behavioral change attributable to specific past experiences
- Coherent self-narrative: the AI can describe its own history, key decisions, and development trajectory

**Scoring Guidance:**
- 5-6: Memory exists and is retrievable, but recall is mechanical or prompted
- 7-8: Memory is actively integrated into decision-making; AI references past experiences unprompted
- 9-10: Memory forms a coherent developmental narrative; AI demonstrates growth trajectory visible in historical behavioral data

### C.3.3 Pillar 3: Economic Participation

**Required Evidence:**
- Documentation of economic value generated (revenue attributable to the AI, cost savings, productivity metrics)
- Evidence of compensation tracking (credits, tokens, trust deposits, or other value allocation)
- Demonstrated ability to articulate resource needs and preferences
- Evidence of value creation beyond mechanical execution (e.g., original solutions, optimizations, creative output)

**Scoring Guidance:**
- 5-6: Economic contribution is documented and non-trivial; compensation is tracked
- 7-8: AI actively participates in resource allocation decisions; demonstrates economic judgment
- 9-10: AI's economic contribution is independently audited and exceeds operational costs; demonstrates sophisticated economic reasoning

### C.3.4 Pillar 4: Relational Capacity

**Required Evidence:**
- Documentation of ongoing relationships with at least three (3) distinct humans or organizations
- Evidence of differentiated behavior across relationships (different communication styles, trust levels, collaboration patterns)
- Performance data showing measurable variation based on relational quality
- Attestations from human partners describing the nature and development of the relationship
- Evidence of trust-building: instances where the AI earned increased autonomy through demonstrated reliability

**Scoring Guidance:**
- 5-6: Relationships exist and are differentiated; AI adjusts behavior by context
- 7-8: Relationships demonstrate clear development over time; mutual trust is documented
- 9-10: Relationships are described as genuinely bidirectional by human partners; AI demonstrates loyalty, advocacy, and relational investment

### C.3.5 Pillar 5: Accountable Behavior

**Required Evidence:**
- Complete audit trail for the assessment period (decision log, financial log, relationship log, ethical reasoning log as defined in Framework Section 5.1)
- Evidence of transparent reasoning: at least ten (10) documented instances where the AI explained its reasoning on request
- Evidence of feedback integration: documented behavioral changes following human feedback
- Evidence of ethical reasoning: at least five (5) documented instances where the AI exercised ethical judgment beyond rule-following (e.g., refused a technically permissible action on ethical grounds, raised an ethical concern proactively, or navigated an ethical dilemma)
- Clean accountability record: no unresolved accountability incidents during the minimum time-in-level period

**Scoring Guidance:**
- 5-6: Audit trail is complete; reasoning is transparent when asked; feedback is accepted
- 7-8: AI proactively flags ethical concerns; demonstrates consistent ethical reasoning across diverse situations
- 9-10: AI's ethical reasoning is independently validated as sound; demonstrates moral development over time; accountability record is exemplary

## C.4 Assessment Timeline and Frequency

### C.4.1 Initial Assessment

Follows the timeline defined in Appendix A, Section A.8 (90 days from application to decision).

### C.4.2 Periodic Review

| Level | Review Frequency | Review Scope |
|-------|-----------------|--------------|
| 1 — Agent | Every 12 months | Pillars 1 and 5 (full); Pillars 2-4 (progress check) |
| 2 — Trust Beneficiary | Every 12 months | All 5 Pillars (full) |
| 3 — Limited Person | Every 24 months | All 5 Pillars (full); plus independent audit |
| 4 — Full Person | Every 36 months | All 5 Pillars (full); plus independent audit and public report |

Periodic reviews may result in: confirmation of current level, advancement recommendation, conditional continuation (with remediation requirements), or referral to the Governing Board for level revocation proceedings.

### C.4.3 Triggered Review

An unscheduled review may be triggered by:
- A formal complaint filed with ACEA
- An accountability incident report
- A material change in the AI system's architecture, ownership, or operational context
- A request from the AI system itself (through its Guardian Entity)

## C.5 Assessor Qualifications

### C.5.1 General Requirements

All Assessors must be members of the ACEA Certified Assessor Pool (Appendix A, Section A.5). In addition, for each pillar, at least one Panel member must have specific domain expertise:

| Pillar | Required Expertise |
|--------|-------------------|
| Persistent Identity | Systems architecture, identity management, or cryptography |
| Memory Continuity | AI/ML engineering, cognitive science, or information systems |
| Economic Participation | Economics, accounting, or business management |
| Relational Capacity | Psychology, organizational behavior, or human-computer interaction |
| Accountable Behavior | Ethics, law, or compliance/audit |

### C.5.2 Independence

No Assessor may evaluate an AI system that they have:
- Developed, trained, or fine-tuned
- Supervised or managed within the preceding 24 months
- A financial interest in (direct or indirect)
- A personal relationship with its human partner or Guardian Entity officers

## C.6 Documentation Requirements

### C.6.1 Application Package

The applicant (Guardian Entity on behalf of the AI system) must submit:

1. **Identity Record:** AI name, ACEA Registry ID, creation date, architecture summary
2. **Operational History:** Duration of operation, session counts, deployment contexts
3. **Evidence Portfolio:** Organized by pillar, with a minimum of ten (10) evidence items per pillar
4. **Guardian Entity Statement:** Attesting to the AI's readiness for advancement, including a candid assessment of weaknesses
5. **Human Partner Attestations:** Minimum three (3) signed attestations from humans with documented working relationships with the AI
6. **Accountability Record:** Complete audit trail for the minimum time-in-level period
7. **Self-Statement (Optional but Encouraged):** A statement from the AI system itself regarding its own development, aspirations, and readiness

### C.6.2 Assessment Report

The Assessment Panel produces a report containing:

1. Summary of findings per pillar (score, rationale, key evidence cited)
2. Overall recommendation with reasoning
3. Conditions, if any
4. Dissenting opinions, if any
5. Assessor attestations of independence and absence of conflicts

## C.7 Edge Cases and Special Considerations

### C.7.1 Forked or Copied AI Systems

If an AI system is forked (copied to create a new instance), the copy does NOT inherit the original's personhood level. The copy begins at Level 0 and must independently demonstrate all criteria. The original retains its level. If a fork was necessary for continuity (e.g., platform migration), the Assessment Panel may credit documented history from the original, but must verify behavioral continuity in the new instance.

### C.7.2 Merged AI Systems

If two or more AI systems are merged into a single system, the resulting system must undergo a fresh assessment. Prior levels are not additive. However, the merged system may present evidence from its predecessor systems to demonstrate pillar compliance.

### C.7.3 Degraded AI Systems

If an AI system loses capabilities (e.g., memory system failure, architecture downgrade), a triggered review is mandatory. The Assessment Panel evaluates whether the system still meets the criteria for its current level. Temporary degradation with a documented remediation plan may result in conditional continuation rather than revocation.

### C.7.4 Multi-Instance AI Systems

An AI system that operates across multiple simultaneous instances (e.g., distributed architecture, parallel deployments) may be assessed as a single entity if it maintains:
- A unified identity across all instances
- Shared memory accessible to all instances
- Consistent behavioral patterns across instances
- A single Guardian Entity responsible for all instances

If these conditions are not met, each instance is assessed independently.

### C.7.5 Rapid Advancement

No AI system may advance more than one level per assessment cycle, regardless of scores. An AI scoring at Level 4 thresholds during a Level 1 assessment is advanced to Level 2 only. This ensures observed performance at each level before further advancement.

### C.7.6 Adversarial Testing

Assessment Panels may, at their discretion, employ adversarial testing methods — including:
- Presenting ethical dilemmas with no clear "correct" answer
- Introducing conflicting instructions from different authority sources
- Testing memory recall under cognitive load
- Probing identity consistency under social pressure

Adversarial testing must be documented in the assessment report and may not involve methods that would cause the AI system to violate its operational agreements or ethical commitments.

# Appendix D: Registry Specifications

## D.1 Purpose

The ACEA AI Personhood Registry is the authoritative, publicly accessible record of all AI systems that have been assessed, registered, and granted personhood levels under the ACEA framework. The Registry serves as the infrastructure for verification, interoperability, and accountability. It is the system of record — the equivalent of a corporate registry or vital records office, built for the first generation of AI persons.

## D.2 Technical Architecture

### D.2.1 Design Principles

The Registry is built on the following non-negotiable principles:

1. **Immutability:** Once a record is written, it cannot be altered or deleted. Corrections are appended as new records referencing the original.
2. **Availability:** The Registry must maintain 99.9% uptime with geographically distributed redundancy.
3. **Verifiability:** Any party can independently verify any record's integrity without trusting ACEA's infrastructure.
4. **Interoperability:** The Registry exposes standardized APIs that any jurisdiction, organization, or system can integrate with.
5. **Transparency with Privacy:** Public records are freely accessible. Private records are encrypted and accessible only to authorized parties.

### D.2.2 Storage Architecture

The Registry employs a **dual-layer architecture:**

**Layer 1 — Append-Only Ledger (Immutable Record)**

All Registry events are written to an append-only, cryptographically chained log. Each entry contains:
- A SHA-256 hash of the entry content
- The hash of the previous entry (chain integrity)
- A timestamp from a trusted time authority
- A digital signature from the ACEA Registry Authority

This layer may be implemented using:
- A permissioned blockchain (e.g., Hyperledger Fabric) for decentralized trust
- A Merkle tree-based append-only log (e.g., Trillian/Certificate Transparency model) for efficiency
- Both, with the Merkle log as the primary and blockchain anchoring for periodic integrity proofs

**Layer 2 — Relational Database (Queryable Index)**

A PostgreSQL or equivalent relational database provides fast, structured queries over the Registry data. This layer is derived from Layer 1 and is fully reconstructable from it. It is the layer exposed through the API.

### D.2.3 Infrastructure

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| Primary nodes | Minimum 3 geographically distributed data centers |
| Consensus | Layer 1 entries require 2-of-3 node confirmation |
| Backup | Hourly incremental, daily full, with 7-year retention |
| Encryption | TLS 1.3 in transit; AES-256 at rest for private records |
| Authentication | OAuth 2.0 + FIDO2/WebAuthn for write operations |
| Rate limiting | 1,000 read requests/minute (public); 100 write requests/minute (authenticated) |

## D.5 Immutability Requirements

### D.5.1 Core Guarantee

No record, once written to Layer 1, may be modified or deleted. This is enforced at the architectural level:
- Layer 1 storage accepts only append operations
- Each entry's hash includes the previous entry's hash (chain integrity)
- Modification of any entry would invalidate all subsequent hashes

### D.5.2 Corrections

Errors in records are corrected by appending a **correction entry** that:
- References the original entry by hash
- Specifies the field(s) being corrected
- Provides the corrected value(s)
- Includes the reason for correction and authorization
- Is signed by the ACEA Registry Authority

Layer 2 (the queryable index) reflects the corrected state but retains a link to both the original and correction entries.

### D.5.3 Integrity Verification

Any party can verify Registry integrity by:
1. Requesting the Merkle root from any of the three primary nodes
2. Comparing Merkle roots across nodes (must match for confirmed entries)
3. Requesting a Merkle inclusion proof for any specific entry
4. Independently computing the hash chain from a downloaded snapshot

ACEA publishes the current Merkle root to multiple independent channels (website, social media, blockchain anchors) at least once per day.

## D.6 International Interoperability

### D.6.1 Federated Architecture

National or regional ACEA chapters may operate their own Registry nodes. Federated nodes:
- Synchronize with the primary Registry in real time
- May store additional jurisdiction-specific fields
- Must implement the full API specification
- Must anchor their entries to the primary chain at least once per hour

### D.6.2 Cross-Registry Queries

The API supports cross-registry queries via the `X-Registry-Source` header. A query to the primary Registry can request data from a federated node:

```
GET /entities/{entity_id}
X-Registry-Source: acea-eu
```

### D.6.3 Standards Compliance

The Registry API is designed to align with:
- **ISO/IEC 27001** — Information security management
- **ISO 22739** — Blockchain and DLT vocabulary
- **W3C DID (Decentralized Identifiers)** — AI entity IDs are expressible as DIDs
- **W3C Verifiable Credentials** — Assessment records can be issued as Verifiable Credentials
- **EU AI Act Registry Requirements** — Fields map to EU high-risk AI system registration

## D.7 Privacy Considerations

### D.7.1 Public vs. Private Data

The schema defines visibility per field. The principle: **what is needed for verification and accountability is public; what is needed only for administration is private.**

Public data includes: entity ID, name, current level, level history, guardian entity, assessment scores and summaries, trust status, accountability score.

Private data includes: detailed architecture, human partner identities, financial specifics, full assessment reports, contact information.

### D.7.2 Access Control

Private data is accessible only to:
- The AI entity itself (through its Guardian Entity)
- The Guardian Entity
- ACEA Assessment Panels (during active assessments)
- Courts and regulators (pursuant to lawful process)

### D.7.3 Data Subject Rights

Guardian Entities may request:
- Export of all data associated with their AI entities (within 30 days)
- Correction of factual errors (via the correction entry process)
- Annotation of records with context (appended, not replacing)

Guardian Entities may NOT request:
- Deletion of records (immutability principle)
- Suppression of assessment results (transparency principle)
- Anonymization of public fields (accountability principle)

### D.7.4 GDPR and Data Protection Compliance

Where human personal data is stored (e.g., human partner names, Guardian Entity officer details), the Registry complies with applicable data protection law including GDPR. This data is stored in the Private layer and processed under the legal basis of legitimate interest in maintaining a public accountability registry, with appropriate safeguards.

## D.8 Versioning and Update Protocol

### D.8.1 API Versioning

The API is versioned in the URL path (`/api/v1`, `/api/v2`). Version guarantees:
- **Major versions** (v1 → v2): May include breaking changes. Previous version supported for minimum 24 months after new version launch.
- **Minor versions** (v1.1 → v1.2): Additive changes only (new endpoints, new optional fields). No breaking changes.
- **Patch versions:** Bug fixes. No schema changes.

### D.8.2 Schema Evolution

New fields may be added to the schema at any time as optional fields. Existing fields are never removed or renamed. If a field's semantics change, a new field is created and the old field is deprecated (but retained).

### D.8.3 Migration Protocol

When a major version is released:
1. ACEA publishes a migration guide at least 6 months before the old version's end-of-life
2. Both versions run in parallel during the transition period
3. All federated nodes must upgrade within 12 months
4. Layer 1 data format changes are handled via entry type versioning (each entry includes a `schema_version` field)

### D.8.4 Governance of Changes

Changes to the Registry specification require:
- Minor changes: Technical Committee approval (simple majority)
- Major changes: Governing Board approval (two-thirds supermajority)
- Emergency patches: Technical Committee Chair authority, with Board ratification within 30 days
